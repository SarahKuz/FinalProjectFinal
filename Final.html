Preparing for The Storm

Can NYC Survive Another Sandy?

By Sarah A. Kusnetz

Its been nearly 6 years since Super Storm Sandy hit New York

Sandy revealed that much of NYC's infastructure is vulnerable to rising waters and future storms. In fact, there were over 90,000 buildings in the flood zones when Sandy hit. As a result there was $23 billion was spent on recovery efforts. As the negative effects of climate change continue to plauge our coast lines, one would expect NYC to halt or mitigate its waterfront developments. However, our most low-lying waterfronts, such as Red Hook and Lower-Manhattan, have continued to see massive amounts of development.

If You Build It Will It Flood?

This image was taken just days after Sandy in Red Hook. Image courtesy of Michael Fleshman

As part of the Red Hook Integrated Flood Protection plan the city will install a semi-permanent Beard Street barrier wall that will stand four feet tall and protect the neighborhood from future mild storm surge. While the wall won’t protect against another 100-year storm like Sandy, it will be effective in preventing flooding from a ten-year storm, or less threatening storms.

"If it was a Sandy-level storm, it would not protect it," said Jessica Colon, a senior policy advisor for the Mayor's Office of Recovery and Resiliency "Red Hook also floods from underneath. It can bubble up on the dry side of the wall. We don't understand that fully, but storm surge can push up the groundwater table," which is only 5- to 10-feet below the surface throughout Red Hook.

Living in a Floodplain

The above map depicts the the 100-Year Floodplain for the 2020s based on FEMA's Preliminary Work Map data and the New York Panel on Climate Change. The shaded area on the map shows the 90th Percentile Projections for a Sea-Level Rise of 11 inches. From this image it is clear that within 100 years the entirety of Red Hook will be underwater, as will a large portion of lower Manhattan.

Yet, even with the constant threat of flooding, the low-lying areas of Red Hook and lower Manhattan have seen unprecidented devlopment since the 2012 superstorm. In fact, according to Grant Long, StreetEasy's senior economist, “The demand for new housing has proven stronger than the memory of Sandy’s destruction.”

In the six years since seawater inundated the lobbies of lower Manhattan's luxury apartment towers, nine new developments have broken ground in the very same flood zone. Since the storm, 27 new residential buildings have been constructed in Red Hook, with all but two located in flood zones.

In the chart above we see the average prices of homes in both Red Hook and Lower Manhattan from 2011 through 2017. The data, from streeteasy open data, clearly shows that after Super Storm Sandy people were not only deterred by inevitable flooding but continued to buy at increased prices.
